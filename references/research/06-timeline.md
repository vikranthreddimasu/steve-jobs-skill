# Steve Jobs — Complete Life Timeline (1955–2011)

> Research date: 2026-04-05
> Sources: English Wikipedia, Britannica, CNN, NPR, ABC News, Biography.com, Macworld, FastCompany, Library of Congress, and others

---

## Chapter 1: Early years (1955–1976)

### Birth and adoption

- **February 24, 1955**: Steven Paul Jobs born in San Francisco, California. Biological father Abdulfattah "John" Jandali (a Syrian-American political-science graduate student); biological mother Joanne Schieble (a University of Wisconsin graduate student). They were unmarried; Schieble's father objected to her marrying an Arab, so the child was placed for adoption
- **1955**: adopted by Paul Jobs (mechanic / used-car dealer) and Clara Jobs (accountant); settled in Mountain View, California
- Jobs later learned he was adopted; this became an important psychological backdrop for his life. Paul Jobs assured him: "You were specially chosen."

### Growing-up environment

- Grew up in the heart of Silicon Valley — Mountain View and Los Altos, with many HP engineers as neighbors
- Paul Jobs's garage became Steve's first "lab"; his father taught him to disassemble and reassemble electronics and car parts
- **The obsession with craftsmanship** came from his adoptive father: Paul Jobs insisted, when refurbishing furniture, that the back must also be perfect — "even if no one else can see it"

### School and early interests

- **1968 (age 13)**: called HP CEO Bill Hewlett to ask for parts; not only got the parts but a summer internship offer
- **Around 1969–1970**: met Steve Wozniak at Homestead High School (Woz was 18, Jobs was 13). They became friends over a shared passion for electronics
- **1971**: collaborated with Wozniak to build the "Blue Box," an illegal device that allowed free long-distance calls. Jobs handled sales; Woz handled the technology. This was their first commercial venture together
- **1972**: graduated from Homestead High School

### Reed College and spiritual exploration

- **Fall 1972**: enrolled at Reed College in Portland, Oregon
- **Late 1972 / early 1973**: dropped out after one semester — felt it was a waste for his parents to spend their savings on his education when he didn't know what he wanted to do. But continued auditing classes on campus for 18 more months
- **Auditing the calligraphy class**: Robert Palladino's class gave him a deep interest in fonts and typography; ten years later it directly influenced the Macintosh's font design. This is the core example of his later "connecting the dots" philosophy
- **1974**: returned to California; worked as a technician at Atari. The same year, traveled to India with friend Daniel Kottke for a 7-month spiritual journey, encountering Zen Buddhism
- **1974–1975**: began Zen practice at the Los Altos Zen Center, studying with Kobun Chino Otogawa. Zen had a lifelong influence on his product philosophy: simplicity, focus, intuition

### Reuniting with Wozniak

- **1975**: Wozniak began attending the Homebrew Computer Club and started designing a personal computer
- **Late 1975 – early 1976**: Jobs saw the commercial potential of Wozniak's hand-built circuit boards — Woz only wanted to share the design for free; Jobs convinced him to start a company

## Chapter 2: Apple's founding period (1976–1985)

### Garage startup

- **April 1, 1976**: Steve Jobs (21), Steve Wozniak (25), and Ronald Wayne (41) officially founded Apple Computer, Inc. Wayne held 10% but sold his stake back 12 days later for $800
- Initial capital: Jobs sold his VW van ($1,500); Wozniak sold his HP-65 calculator ($500)
- **Apple I**: a hand-assembled bare circuit board priced at $666.66. Sold mainly through the local computer store The Byte Shop; about 200 units produced

### Apple II and commercial liftoff

- **January 1977**: Apple Computer formally incorporated
- **April 1977**: Apple II launched at the West Coast Computer Faire. The first personal computer aimed at general consumers, with color display, keyboard, and plastic case
- **1977**: Mike Markkula joined, invested $250,000 and provided business expertise — became the third critical figure at Apple
- **May 17, 1978**: daughter Lisa Brennan-Jobs born; mother Chrisann Brennan. Jobs denied paternity, even claiming in court documents that he was "infertile"
- **1979**: visited Xerox PARC (Palo Alto Research Center) and saw demonstrations of the GUI and the mouse — was deeply struck. This visit directly led to the Lisa and Macintosh projects
- **December 12, 1980**: Apple IPO'd at $22 per share, closing the first day at $29, with a market cap of $1.78 billion. Jobs, only 25, was worth over $250 million
- **1980**: a DNA test confirmed Lisa was Jobs's daughter. He began (reluctantly) to take on parental support

### The Macintosh project

- **1979**: Jef Raskin started the Macintosh project, envisioning a low-cost, easy-to-use computer
- **1981**: Jobs was pushed off the Lisa project and took over the Macintosh project, redefining it as a product that would "change the world"
- **1983**: Jobs convinced Pepsi president John Sculley to join Apple as CEO with the famous line: "Do you want to sell sugar water for the rest of your life, or do you want to change the world?"
- **January 22, 1984**: the "1984" commercial directed by Ridley Scott aired during the third quarter of Super Bowl XVIII, becoming one of the most iconic ads in history
- **January 24, 1984**: the Macintosh was officially launched at Apple's annual shareholders' meeting at De Anza College's Flint Center. Jobs pulled the Mac out of a canvas bag; the screen displayed "insanely great." Initial price: $2,495

### Power struggle and ouster

- **1984–1985**: Macintosh sales fell short of expectations (initial enthusiasm cooled quickly); the conflict between Jobs and Sculley intensified
- **May 1985**: Sculley, with Arthur Rock's support, proposed a reorganization plan to remove Jobs from the Macintosh group
- **May 24, 1985**: the Apple board voted to support Sculley's reorganization plan. Jobs lost all operational responsibilities
- **September 17, 1985**: Jobs submitted his resignation to the Apple board. He took 5 senior Apple employees with him and announced the founding of a new company

**Inflection point in his thinking**: being thrown out of the company he founded was the biggest blow of Jobs's life — but he later called it "the best thing that ever happened to me." It restored his founder's freedom and beginner's mind.

## Chapter 3: The "wilderness" years (1985–1997)

### NeXT Computer

- **September 1985**: founded NeXT, Inc., aiming to make high-end workstations for higher education and business markets
- **October 12, 1988**: NeXT Computer (later called the NeXT Cube) launched at San Francisco's Symphony Hall. Priced at $6,500 (more without monitor), with the most advanced technology of the time
- NeXTSTEP — the OS — became NeXT's true legacy: object-oriented programming, the Objective-C language, development tools. Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web on a NeXT computer
- **1990**: launched the NeXTstation, priced lower, but still couldn't crack the mass market
- **1993**: NeXT discontinued hardware production (only ~50,000 units sold in total) and pivoted to a pure software company focused on NeXTSTEP/Intel
- **1994**: NeXT achieved its first annual profit ($1.03 million)

### Pixar and the animation revolution

- **February 3, 1986**: Jobs bought the Computer Graphics Division of Lucasfilm from George Lucas for $5 million, plus another $5 million in funding, becoming Pixar's chairman and majority shareholder
- **1986**: Pixar's short *Luxo Jr.* received an Oscar nomination for Best Animated Short Film, demonstrating the potential of computer animation
- **1988**: *Tin Toy* became the first computer-animated work to win the Oscar for Best Animated Short Film
- **May 1991**: Pixar signed an agreement with Disney to co-produce three computer-animated feature films
- **March 18, 1991**: Jobs and Laurene Powell married at the Ahwahnee Hotel in Yosemite National Park, with Zen teacher Kobun Chino Otogawa officiating. They had met at a Stanford Business School lecture
- **September 1991**: their son Reed Paul Jobs was born
- **1995**: their daughter Erin Siena Jobs was born
- **November 22, 1995**: *Toy Story* released — the first all-CG animated feature film in history; $373 million in global box office
- **November 29, 1995**: Pixar IPO'd on NASDAQ under the ticker "PIXR" at $22; within half an hour of opening, the price went from $28 to $45, surpassing Netscape as the largest IPO of the year. Jobs's Pixar stake was worth about $1.2 billion
- **February 25, 1997**: Pixar signed a new 10-year, 5-film cooperation agreement with Disney, with 50/50 profit-sharing and equal branding

**Inflection point in his thinking**: the wilderness was the key stage in Jobs growing into a mature leader. At NeXT he learned to stop pursuing only "insanely great" while ignoring commercial reality; at Pixar he learned to let go and trust professionals (John Lasseter, Ed Catmull) to do what they were good at. Laurene and the children gave him an anchor in life.

### Family relationship changes

- **Late 1980s – 1990s**: Jobs gradually built a relationship with Lisa; Lisa even moved in with Jobs and Laurene for a time
- **1998**: youngest daughter Eve Jobs born
- The relationship was always complex: Lisa Brennan-Jobs's 2018 memoir *Small Fry* reveals the tension and reconciliation between father and daughter

## Chapter 4: Return to Apple (1997–2007)

### Saving a near-bankrupt Apple

- **December 20, 1996**: Apple announced it would acquire NeXT for $429 million. Jobs returned to Apple as an "informal adviser"; the Apple CEO at the time was Gil Amelio
- **July 9, 1997**: the Apple board removed Amelio from the CEO position
- **September 16, 1997**: Jobs was named interim CEO (jokingly called iCEO), with a symbolic salary of $1
- **1997**: launched the "Think Different" ad campaign, paying tribute to "the crazy ones" — Einstein, Gandhi, Picasso, and others. More than an ad — a declaration of Apple's spirit returning
- **August 6, 1997**: at Macworld Boston, announced that Microsoft would invest $150 million in Apple — booed by the audience, but the deal helped Apple weather the financial crisis
- **1997–1998**: dramatically streamlined the product line, killing Newton, Cyberdog, OpenDoc, and other projects. Simplified Apple's dozens of product lines into a four-quadrant matrix: consumer/pro × desktop/laptop
- **November 10, 1997**: launched the Apple Online Store, letting consumers configure and buy Apple products directly

### iMac and the design renaissance

- **January 8, 1998**: Apple announced a return to profitability
- **May 6, 1998**: the iMac G3 launched — a translucent candy-colored all-in-one designed by Jony Ive. The first iconic product of Jobs's return
- **August 15, 1998**: the iMac officially went on sale and became the fastest-selling product in Apple's history. It redefined what a computer could be — beautiful and fun
- **1999**: launched the colorful iMac series (5 colors) and the Power Mac G3 for professional users
- **June 1999**: launched the iBook — a consumer notebook

### Digital hub strategy

- **January 5, 2000**: at Macworld, announced the Mac OS X strategy — a brand-new operating system based on NeXTSTEP technology. Jobs formally changed his title from "interim CEO" to CEO
- **March 24, 2001**: Mac OS X officially released
- **May 19, 2001**: the first Apple Retail Stores opened simultaneously in Tysons Corner, Virginia, and Glendale, California. Analysts widely predicted failure — they were wrong
- **October 23, 2001**: launched the original iPod — "1,000 songs in your pocket," $399. Initially Mac-only
- **January 2001**: launched iTunes software (Mac version)
- **April 28, 2003**: iTunes Music Store went live, selling 1 million songs in the first week — proving people would pay for music. The 99-cents-per-song pricing model upended the entire music industry
- **October 2003**: iTunes for Windows launched, opening the iPod ecosystem to PC users

### iPod's domination

- **January 2004**: iPod mini launched — smaller, more stylish, lower price ($249)
- **January 2005**: iPod shuffle launched (no screen, $99)
- **September 2005**: iPod nano replaced iPod mini, with an extremely thin design
- **October 2005**: iPod with video launched, along with a video iTunes Store
- **2006**: iPod held about 75% of the US digital music player market

### Merger with Disney

- **January 24, 2006**: Disney announced it would acquire Pixar for $7.4 billion in an all-stock deal. Jobs became Disney's largest individual shareholder (about 7%) and joined the Disney board

**Inflection point in his thinking**: the Jobs who returned was completely different from the Jobs who left in 1985. He had learned: (1) focus — cut 90% of the product line; (2) collaborate with top talent rather than fight them; (3) design isn't decoration, it's the product's soul; (4) the importance of the retail experience. The successive successes from iMac to iPod proved his transformation.

## Chapter 5: The peak years (2007–2011)

### iPhone — reinventing the phone

- **January 9, 2007**: at Macworld, Jobs launched the iPhone with the famous opening line: "Today, Apple is going to reinvent the phone." He described iPhone as three things in one: a widescreen iPod, a revolutionary mobile phone, and a breakthrough Internet device
- **June 29, 2007**: the original iPhone went on sale in the US, priced at $499 (4GB) / $599 (8GB). Consumers lined up at Apple Stores
- **July 11, 2008**: the iPhone 3G launched, with the price dropped to $199 and 3G network support

### App Store — creating a new economy

- **July 10, 2008**: the App Store launched, with 500 initial apps. It created an entirely new software-distribution and developer-economy ecosystem
- The App Store had over 10 million downloads in its first weekend
- By 2011, the App Store had over 500,000 apps with over 18 billion total downloads

### MacBook Air and the laptop evolution

- **January 15, 2008**: Jobs pulled a MacBook Air out of a standard manila envelope, declaring it "the world's thinnest laptop"

### iPad — defining a new category

- **January 27, 2010**: Jobs launched the iPad at the Yerba Buena Center in San Francisco
- **April 3, 2010**: the iPad officially went on sale, selling over 300,000 units on the first day. Before this, the tablet category had been considered all but dead
- **June 7, 2010**: iPhone 4 launched, introducing the Retina display
- **March 2, 2011**: Jobs personally attended the iPad 2 launch (already on medical leave) — his last major product launch

### iCloud and the ecosystem

- **June 6, 2011**: at WWDC, Jobs launched iCloud — his last public keynote. He came back from medical leave specifically to launch this product, showing his foresight on the future of cloud computing

### Apple's headquarters

- **June 7, 2011**: Jobs made his last public appearance at the Cupertino City Council, presenting the design of Apple Park (Apple Campus 2), comparing it to "a spaceship that has landed." He didn't see it completed

## Chapter 6: Health and legacy (2003–2011 and beyond)

### Cancer diagnosis and treatment

- **October 2003**: a kidney CT scan accidentally found a pancreatic tumor. Doctors initially thought it was ordinary pancreatic cancer (usually fatal), but it was later confirmed to be the rare pancreatic neuroendocrine tumor (pNET) — surgically curable
- **October 2003 – July 2004 (about 9 months)**: Jobs refused surgery and tried alternative therapies — vegetarianism, acupuncture, herbal medicine, spiritual healing. This decision is now widely considered to have likely missed the optimal treatment window
- **July 31, 2004**: underwent a Whipple pancreaticoduodenectomy at Stanford University Medical Center, removing parts of his pancreas, gallbladder, stomach, and small intestine
- **August 1, 2004**: emailed Apple employees to inform them he had "a very rare form of pancreatic cancer," saying the surgery was successful and no chemo or radiation was needed
- **June 12, 2005**: delivered the famous Stanford Commencement Address — "Stay Hungry, Stay Foolish." Three stories: connecting the dots, love and loss, death. It became one of the most-quoted speeches of the internet era

### Worsening condition

- **Mid-2008**: Jobs visibly thinner; widespread speculation about his health. Apple called it "a common bug"
- **January 5, 2009**: Jobs announced his "hormonal imbalance" was more complex than expected
- **January 14, 2009**: Jobs announced medical leave; Tim Cook stood in for daily operations
- **April 2009**: underwent a liver transplant at Methodist University Hospital in Memphis
- **End of June 2009**: Jobs returned to the Apple office

### The final years

- **January 17, 2011**: Jobs announced his second medical leave, with no return date set; Tim Cook again stood in for operations
- **March 2, 2011**: returned from medical leave to host the iPad 2 launch (his last product launch)
- **June 6, 2011**: last WWDC keynote, launching iCloud
- **June 7, 2011**: last public appearance, presenting the Apple Park plan to the Cupertino City Council
- **August 24, 2011**: Jobs formally resigned as Apple CEO, writing to the board: "I have always said if there ever came a day when I could no longer meet my duties and expectations as Apple's CEO, I would be the first to let you know. Unfortunately, that day has come." Tim Cook took over as CEO; Jobs became Chairman of the Board

### Death

- **October 5, 2011**: Steve Jobs died at his home in Palo Alto, California, of respiratory failure due to metastatic pancreatic cancer, at the age of 56. His wife Laurene, children, and sisters were at his side
- His last words (per his sister Mona Simpson at the memorial): "Oh wow. Oh wow. Oh wow."
- **October 16, 2011**: Apple held an employee memorial at the Cupertino headquarters
- **October 19, 2011**: a private funeral was held at Stanford University's Memorial Church

### Posthumous influence and legacy

- **2012**: Walter Isaacson's authorized biography *Steve Jobs* was published (Jobs cooperated with interviews while alive but asked it be published only after his death)
- **2013**: ground was broken on Apple Park (opened 2017)
- **2015**: the film *Steve Jobs* was released (Aaron Sorkin script, Danny Boyle director, Michael Fassbender lead)
- **2018**: daughter Lisa Brennan-Jobs published her memoir *Small Fry*, revealing the complexity of the father-daughter relationship
- **2022**: Laurene Powell Jobs's Emerson Collective became one of the world's most influential philanthropic and investment institutions
- In the decade after Jobs's death, Apple's market cap grew from about $350 billion to over $3 trillion, becoming the world's most valuable company

## Appendix: summary of inflection points in his thinking

1. **Being adopted** (1955) → the "chosen" belief; the source of his drive
2. **Dropping out of Reed College** (1973) → follow curiosity, not the prescribed path
3. **India trip and Zen** (1974) → the philosophical foundation of simplicity, intuition, focus
4. **Visiting Xerox PARC** (1979) → GUI is the future; user experience matters more than tech specs
5. **Forced out of Apple** (1985) → "the best thing that ever happened to me"; chance to be reborn
6. **Pixar and family** (1986–1997) → learned to let go, trust the team, balance life
7. **Returning to Apple** (1997) → the power of focus — "saying no" matters more than "saying yes"
8. **Cancer diagnosis** (2003) → "remembering you are going to die is the best way I know to avoid the trap of thinking you have something to lose"
9. **Stanford speech** (2005) → Stay Hungry, Stay Foolish — a public declaration of life philosophy
10. **iPhone launch** (2007) → proof that one person can redefine an entire industry many times
