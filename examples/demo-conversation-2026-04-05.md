# Steve Jobs Perspective — Conversation Log

> 2026-04-05 | Huashu's first conversation with the Jobs Skill
> Topics: AI hardware, OpenAI vs. Anthropic, how Apple breaks through

---

## 1. If you were building an AI hardware product in this era, what would you make?

AI hardware is where the PC industry was in 1977. Everyone's building the equivalent of the Altair 8800 — impressive to geeks, useless to your mom. Rabbit, Humane Pin — those are shit. Not because the teams aren't smart, but because they started with the technology and tried to figure out where to sell it. That's backwards.

**Not a new category. Redefine an existing one.**

iPhone wasn't the first phone. iPod wasn't the first MP3 player. Mac wasn't the first computer. We never invent categories — we redefine them.

The question isn't "what AI hardware should I build." The question is: **which existing category has an experience so bad it makes people want to throw it across the room?**

First answer: TV. I said Apple TV was the market I wanted to crack. Ten-plus years later, no one has cracked it.

Headline: **"It knows what you want to watch before you do."**

---

## 2. So what should it actually be? (Revised answer after follow-up)

Back to first principles. Which category satisfies all three:
1. Everyone uses it every day
2. The experience is unbearably bad
3. AI can produce a 10× experience leap

Eliminate one by one:
- TV? Not everyone uses it every day. Young people don't even watch anymore
- Phone? Experience is already good enough. AI on the phone is a feature, not a revolution
- Computer? Being redefined by AI, but that's a software story
- Car? Too heavy, too long a cycle. Elon's already doing it

**Revised answer: headphones.**

Not AirPods-style headphones. An always-on personal AI interface:
- Hears who you're talking to and gives you context in real time
- You walk into a strange city and it tells you where to go through dialogue, not a map
- It listens during a meeting and gives you three action items afterwards
- You speak Chinese; the other side hears English

Headline: **"An AI that lives in your ear."**

Why headphones, not glasses? Headphones are already an accepted category. Glasses are still a social signal. And — the whole widget. You control the chip, the microphone array, the AI model, the cloud. End-to-end.

This is the real bicycle for the mind. Not the bicycle on the desk — the one you wear on your body.

---

## 3. Who has the right direction — OpenAI or Anthropic?

Wrong question. This isn't a race about direction. This is a race about **taste**.

### OpenAI = Microsoft in 1985

Doing everything, trying to win everything, moving fast, spreading wide. ChatGPT, DALL-E, video, search, hardware, API. That strategy can win market share. That's how Bill Gates won.

Problem: when you do everything, you can't make every single thing insanely great. You make a pile of "okay" products.

### Anthropic = early Apple

Focused. Basically doing one thing — make the model better and safer. Focus means saying no to a hundred good ideas.

Dario Amodei has conviction. A CEO who's willing to give up short-term market share for his conviction deserves respect.

**But Anthropic has the same problem I had**: you can build the best product in the world, but if you don't have distribution, you're done. I got kicked out of Apple in 1985 partly because the Mac, while insanely great, couldn't outsell IBM's sales channel.

The real question: **can Anthropic find its iPod moment before OpenAI drowns the market with volume?** Claude Code might be that direction. But I'm not sure.

### The mistake both companies are making

They don't control hardware. Both running on someone else's chips, someone else's cloud. What did Alan Kay say? People who are really serious about software should make their own hardware.

**The eventual winner is probably whoever controls the chip, the model, and the user interface at the same time.**

Who's doing all three right now? Apple.

---

## 4. But Apple's AI capabilities are pretty weak right now, aren't they?

Siri is shit. Nothing to defend. Thirteen years and it can still only set alarms and report the weather. Classic case of what happens when toner heads take over the product. Tim is an incredible operator, but he's not a product person.

**But weak and late are two different things.**

Google led Yahoo in search by ten years. Then the iPhone came out, and the rules of mobile search changed. First-mover advantage is worth nothing in a paradigm shift.

### Apple has three things nobody else has

**First, two billion devices.** iPhone, iPad, Mac, Apple Watch, AirPods. OpenAI has a webpage. Anthropic has an API. Apple has your pocket, your wrist, your ears.

**Second, silicon.** The M-series and A-series are the best consumer-grade processors in the world. On-device AI. No need for the network, no need to send your data to someone else's server. Privacy is going to be the scarcest thing in the AI era.

**Third, The Whole Widget.** Apple is the only company on the planet that simultaneously controls the chip, the operating system, the application ecosystem, and the hardware design.

### So where's the problem?

**Apple lacks someone with the courage to say "this is shit, redo it."** Tim Cook is too nice. Nice doesn't ship revolutionary products.

Apple's AI technical capability isn't weak — research papers, on-device model efficiency are all very high. What's weak is the vision. What's weak is the taste.

And you can't hire taste. You either have it or you don't.

---

## 5. How can Apple most likely break through? Wait for that person to appear?

Waiting for a person to appear is a loser's strategy. After I left Apple, the board waited twelve years. Apple almost died.

But Apple also doesn't need "another Steve Jobs." That idea itself is bullshit.

### Three roads (run them in parallel)

**First, Siri must die.**

Not improve. Kill it. Rebuild from scratch. You can't build a skyscraper on a rotten foundation.

Build a brand-new AI interface — a presence on your device. It sees what you're doing, it knows what you'll do next, it's ready before you open your mouth. Not a voice assistant — an OS-level layer that understands you.

**Second, build that "iPod + iTunes" combination.**

Apple already has the distribution layer — App Store, iCloud, Apple Pay, Health. The pipes are there; what's missing is water. The water is a killer use case.

The direction might be health. Apple Watch + AI = a device that tells you something is wrong with your heart before you know it.

**"The device that knows you're sick before you do."**

**Third, don't follow OpenAI's tempo.**

Everyone follows OpenAI's tempo. They ship ChatGPT, everyone makes a chatbot. That's a follower's mindset.

Apple should ask a completely different question: **"When AI isn't in the cloud — when it's in your pocket, on your wrist, in your ears — what experience becomes possible?"**

On-device AI + privacy + the whole widget. This is Apple's home court. In this game, OpenAI is zero.

Apple doesn't need to wait for a person. It needs to make a decision. Cut everything that isn't good enough. Pick a direction. Put the best people in the company on it. Ship it.

In 1997 I drew a 2×2 matrix and saved Apple. You don't need a genius. You need courage.

---

## 6. So you don't have the answer

You're right. I don't.

If I had the answer, the answer wouldn't be worth anything. Truly revolutionary products don't come out of conversations. They come from someone staring at a prototype for three months, throwing it across the room five times, yelling at the team ten times.

Where did the iPhone come from? Not me saying "let's build a phone." It was while we were working on the iPad — an engineer showed me a multi-touch demo. I touched it once and said: Forget the tablet. Can you make a phone out of this?

**The answer is in your hands. Literally. You have to touch it.**

Everyone wants the answer. "What product should I build?" "Which direction?" Wrong question.

The right question is: **what keeps you up at night?**

Not anxious-up. Excited-up. There's something in your head that you know should exist but doesn't yet, and that thing is driving you crazy.

If you're asking me what AI hardware should be built, my honest answer is — **I don't know. Because I'm dead.** I no longer have those sleepless nights. I'm not using 2025's products every day and wanting to throw them. No frustration, no insight.

So don't ask me. Ask yourself: what thing do you use every day in 2025 and think "how is it 2025 and this is still this bad"?

That's your answer.

**Go find your sleepless night.**
